Long ago, at the edge of a deep forest, there lived a kind couple who wished with all their hearts for a child. One
  spring morning, their wish came true. They had a baby girl with bright eyes and a tiny laugh like bells.

  “She is perfect,” said her mother, rocking her gently.

  “We’ll keep her safe forever,” said her father.

  Near their cottage stood a high stone wall around a mysterious garden. In that garden grew shining green rapunzel
  leaves. The mother had once craved those leaves so badly that she became very weak. Her husband had climbed the wall at
  night to gather them for her.

  But the garden belonged to a powerful enchantress named Dame Gothel.

  “You stole from me,” Dame Gothel had hissed when she caught him. “For that, when your baby is born, she will belong to
  me.”

  The frightened father begged, but the enchantress would not change her mind.

  Years later, when the little girl was still very young, Dame Gothel came for her.

  “Her name will be Rapunzel,” she declared, and took the child away.

  ———

  Dame Gothel raised Rapunzel in a hidden tower in the middle of the forest. The tower had no door—only one high window.
  As Rapunzel grew, her hair grew too: long, golden, and bright as sunlight.

  Every day, Gothel visited and called from below:

  “Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair!”

  Rapunzel would sigh, braid her long hair, and lower it out the window. Gothel would climb up and bring food, books, and
  lessons—but never freedom.

  “Why can’t I go outside?” Rapunzel asked one day.

  “The world is cruel,” Gothel answered. “Only I can protect you.”

  Rapunzel looked through the tiny window at birds flying over the trees. “But birds are safe in the sky.”

  “Birds are foolish,” Gothel snapped. “Now practice your songs.”

  Rapunzel loved to sing. Her voice drifted over the forest like silver music, and the animals would gather to listen.

  ———

  One evening, a young prince named Adrian rode through the forest and heard the song.

  He stopped his horse. “Who is singing?”

  His guard listened. “It sounds like an angel, Your Highness.”

  The prince followed the music until he found the hidden tower. He searched for a door, but there was none.

  “This is strange,” he whispered.

  He returned the next day, hiding behind a tree. At sunset, he saw Dame Gothel approach the tower.

  “Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair!”

  A rope of golden hair dropped from the high window. The prince’s eyes widened as Gothel climbed up and vanished inside.

  “So that is the way in,” he said.

  That night, after Gothel had gone, the prince stepped to the foot of the tower and called softly:

  “Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair!”

  Rapunzel heard a voice unlike Gothel’s—gentle and young. Curious, she lowered her hair. The prince climbed up and
  entered the tower.

  Rapunzel gasped and stepped back. “Who are you?”

  “I’m Prince Adrian,” he said, bowing. “I heard your song and followed it. I did not mean to frighten you.”

  Rapunzel stared at him. “You’re the first person I’ve ever met besides Dame Gothel.”

  The prince looked around the little room. “You live here alone?”

  Rapunzel nodded. “She says it is for my safety.”

  He frowned. “A cage is still a cage, even if someone calls it safe.”

  Rapunzel had never heard anyone speak like that. She looked out the window at the moonlit trees.

  “Tell me about the world,” she whispered.

  So he did. He told her about markets full of laughter, children flying kites, rivers that shone like mirrors, and
  fields of wildflowers. Rapunzel listened with shining eyes.

  Before he left, he said, “May I come again?”

  Rapunzel smiled for the first time in years. “Yes. Please.”

  ———

  Days turned into weeks. The prince visited whenever he could. They talked, laughed, and planned little songs together.
  Rapunzel told him about the stars she watched each night.

  One evening, she said quietly, “I don’t want to stay trapped forever.”

  “Then we’ll find a way out,” Adrian said.

  “How?” asked Rapunzel.

  “I can bring silk rope each visit,” he said. “We can weave a strong ladder. When it’s ready, we’ll climb down and ride
  to my kingdom. You will be truly safe there.”

  Rapunzel nodded bravely. “Let’s do it.”

  Night after night, he brought coils of silk hidden beneath his cloak. Rapunzel wove them into a long rope ladder and
  tucked it under her bed.

  But one afternoon, Rapunzel made a mistake. While Gothel brushed her hair, Rapunzel said without thinking, “You climb
  so much harder than Prince Adrian.”

  Gothel froze. “Prince… Adrian?”

  Rapunzel covered her mouth, but it was too late.

  Gothel’s face turned stormy. “You disobedient girl!”

  She dragged the rope ladder out, cut Rapunzel’s long hair short with a sharp knife, and shouted, “You are not ready for
  freedom!”

  Then she carried Rapunzel away from the tower and left her in a distant cottage by the edge of a desert plain.

  “You will stay here and think about what you’ve done,” Gothel said coldly.

  Rapunzel, frightened but determined, watched the enchantress go. She touched her short hair and whispered, “I am still
  me. I am still brave.”

  ———

  That night, Prince Adrian came to the tower as usual.

  “Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair!”

  A braid dropped from the window. He climbed up quickly—but instead of Rapunzel, he found Dame Gothel.

  “You!” he shouted.

  “Looking for your little songbird?” Gothel sneered. “You’ll never find her.”

  The prince backed away, but in panic he slipped from the window ledge and fell into the thorn bushes below. He
  survived, but his eyes were badly hurt.

  For many months he wandered through forests and fields, calling her name.

  “Rapunzel! Rapunzel!”

  Though he could barely see, he did not give up.

  ———

  Far away, Rapunzel had learned to care for herself in the small cottage. She planted vegetables, fetched water, and
  made friends with nearby shepherd children. She told them stories and sang to them in the evenings.

  “You’re very brave,” one little boy told her.

  Rapunzel smiled. “I’m still learning.”

  One windy afternoon, she heard a voice in the distance calling her name.

  “Rapunzel…”

  Her heart leaped. She ran toward the sound and found Prince Adrian, tired and dusty, leaning on a stick.

  “Adrian!” she cried.

  “Rapunzel?” he whispered.

  She took his hands. “I’m here. I’m here.”

  He reached toward her face and smiled, even through pain. “I found you.”

  Rapunzel’s eyes filled with tears. A few tears fell onto his cheeks—and in that magical moment, his sight began to
  return. The world came into focus slowly: Rapunzel’s face, her short golden hair, and her bright, brave smile.

  “I can see you,” he said in wonder.

  Rapunzel laughed through tears. “And I can finally see my own path.”

  ———

  They rested in the cottage for a few days, with help from the shepherd families. Then they prepared to travel safely to
  the prince’s kingdom.

  “We won’t rush,” Rapunzel said. “We’ll go carefully, village by village.”

  “Together,” Adrian said.

  “Together,” she agreed.

  Along the journey, they spoke with kind farmers, slept in warm inns, and crossed green valleys under the open sky.
  Rapunzel loved every sunrise.

  At last they reached the kingdom gates. The king and queen embraced Adrian with joy and welcomed Rapunzel warmly.

  “You are safe here,” said the queen.

  Rapunzel smiled. “Thank you. But now I know safety is not just walls and locks. Safety is love, truth, and people who
  let you grow.”

  The king nodded. “Wise words.”

  In time, Rapunzel and Adrian married in a cheerful celebration filled with music, flowers, and children dancing in the
  square. Rapunzel sang beneath the open sky, and this time no tower walls held back her voice.

  And from then on, whenever children asked for a story, Rapunzel would tell them this:

  “Be kind. Be brave. And never believe that fear is the same as love.”

  Then she and Adrian would look at each other and smile, grateful that they had found each other—and their way to
  safety.
